    \chapter*{Abstract}
Biological neural circuits remain in functional computational ranges despite substantial variability in synaptic strengths, neuron leak, and inputs. In reservoir computing, performance and stability depend sensitively on hyperparameters such as spectral radius, input scaling, and leak rate; however, it is unclear which network properties govern invariance to these parameters. In this work, we investigate the determinants of hyperparameter invariance using the \textit{C.\ elegans} connectome as a biologically grounded reservoir model.

We quantify computational capability using task-agnostic metrics—Memory Capacity (MC), Information Processing Capacity (IPC), Kernel Rank (KR), and Generalization Rank (GR)—and measure invariance as the coefficient of variation of these metrics under hyperparameter sweeps. We construct controlled perturbations that independently alter connectivity topology, synaptic weight magnitude distributions, and excitatory/inhibitory sign structure while preserving other structural properties. This allows isolation of the causal contributions of topology, magnitude statistics, and global sign balance to computational invariance.

Preliminary results suggest that global sign balance and weight magnitude statistics account for a large fraction of the observed invariance.

\chapter{Echo State Networks}
\section{Echo State Networks: an Introduction}
Echo state networks are discrete time recurrent reservoir computers, introduced by Jaeger \parencite{jaeger_echo_state_2001}. Due the recurrence, echo state networks and are particularly useful in time series analysis\parencite{Jaeger_Schrauwen_2012} and chaotic system prediction problems\parencite{Jager2004}. For readers unfamiliar with reservoir computing, we recommend the tutorial-style introduction\ \parencite{introtoRC} and the accompanying interactive RC Bootcamp notebooks.\parencite{rcbootcampSite}

Reservoir computers have $K$ input units, $N$ internal network (more colloquially known as reservoir network or reservoir) units and L output units. Reservoir computers have 3 sets of weights; input weights$W^{in}_{ij}$ with size $K \times N$, internal weights ($W^{res}_{ij}$) with size $N\times N$, output weights, $W^{out}_{ij}$, with size $N\times L$. \parencite{jaeger_echo_state_2001}. The reservoir acts as a nonlinear expansion and as a memory of the input \parencite{Lukosevicius2012PracticalESN}.

Training a network is the process of tuning its parameters to optimize it for a specific task or set of tasks. When training these networks we train solely $W^{out}_{ij}$, as opposed to training all weights like is typically done in artificial neural networks. Training exclusively the output weights preserves the dynamics of the reservoir while keeping training computationally inexpensive. When training these networks we "tap" in to the dynamics of the "reservoir" by linearly combining different neuron states. Training the recurrent weights would reshape the internal dynamics, often reducing the diversity of activity patterns and thereby decreasing the number of linearly independent features available to the readout layer.

Formally, let $u_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$ be input, $x_t \in \mathbb{R}^{N}$ the reservoir state, and $y_t \in \mathbb{R}^{p}$ the output. With leak $\alpha \in (0,1]$:
\begin{align}
\tilde{x}_{t+1} &= \tanh\!\left(W_{\mathrm{res}} x_t + W_{\mathrm{in}} u_{t+1} + b + \xi_t\right), \\
x_{t+1} &= (1-\alpha)\,x_t + \alpha\,\tilde{x}_{t+1}, \\
y_t &= W_{\mathrm{out}}\,[x_t; u_t; 1],
\end{align}
where $\tanh$ is elementwise, $b$ is not included in our model ,$\xi_t$ is optional i.i.d.\ noise, $W_{\mathrm{res}}\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times N}$ is in our case sparse, and $W_{\mathrm{out}}\in\mathbb{R}^{p\times (N+d+1)}$ is trained.

For this approach to work, our internal network  must satisfy the echo-state property. This property is that asymptotically the state of a reservoir $x(n)$ is uniquely determined by the input history $u_t$ not the initial state of the network ($x_0$). A good way to explain this intuitively a house with a thermostat, imagine two identical houses with the same target temperature ($u_t$) and thermostat program ($W$). Imagine house b starts with a cold temperature ($x_0$) and house a starts with a hot temperature ($x_0'$). With time and a reasonable thermostat program both temperature states ($x_n,x_n'$) will converge and the temperature state ($x_n$) will be determined solely by the temperature target.

\section{Hyper-Parameters}
Echo state networks, like most machine learning models, rely on hyperparameters— parameters specified prior to training that determine the dynamical regime in which the model operates. In reservoir computing, these parameters do not affect $W_{out}$ directly; instead they influence the intrinsic behavior of the reservoir itself, shaping recurrent feedback, external input, and temporal integration.

\textbf{Spectral radius} $\rho(W_{\mathrm{res}})$: Let $W \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$ be a square matrix with eigenvalues 
$\{\lambda_i\}_{i=1}^N$. The \emph{spectral radius} of $W$ is defined as
\[
\rho(W) = \max_{1 \le i \le N} |\lambda_i|.
\]
 The spectral radius roughly controls the strength of recurrent feedback. In the common (heuristic) setting, choosing $\rho(W_{\mathrm{res}}) < 1$ fosters the echo state property by making the reservoir dynamics contractive. Values near $\rho(W_{\mathrm{res}})\approx 1$ typically yield richer, longer-lasting transients while still often preserving the echo state property. When the reservoir is driven by sufficiently strong inputs (non zero inputs in-practice), even $\rho(W_{\mathrm{res}})>1$ can remain effectively stable in practice because the nonlinearity (activation function)(e.g., $\tanh$) reduces the effective gain of the recurrence, damping feedback loops. \parencite{Lukosevicius2012PracticalESN}

A reservoir that does not satisfy the echo state property is not useful for reliable computation. Without the echo state property, the reservoir state is not uniquely determined by the input history; depending largely on the initial condition. In practice, this means that recurrent feedback dominates the dynamics: internal activity grows in magnitude and persists independently of the input; the effect of new inputs is overshadowed. As a result, the reservoir ceases to act as a dynamical system and instead consistently gravitates towards magnitude increasing feedback loops.

 \textbf{Leak rate} $\alpha$: The leak rate controls the intrinsic timescale of the reservoir by interpolating between the previous state and the newly computed activation. The effective recurrence is
\[
x_{t+1} \approx \left[(1-\alpha)x_t + \alpha \tilde{x}_{t+1} \right] ,
\]
 Smaller $\alpha$ increases the persistence of past inputs and extends temporal memory of the reservoir. Larger $\alpha$ causes the system to update more rapidly, reducing memory but increasing responsiveness. Thus, while the spectral radius controls the strength of recurrence, the leak rate controls how quickly that signal dissipates over time.
 
 \textbf{Input scaling} $\|W_{\mathrm{in}}\|$: Input scaling determines the relative strength of external input compared to recurrent feedback. When input scaling is small, reservoir dynamics are dominated by recurrence, and the system primarily reflects its internal structure. Increasing input scaling drives neurons into the nonlinear regime of the activation function, increasing computational richness. However, excessive scaling saturates the activation function, reducing sensitivity to inputs and collapsing the effective dimensionality of the reservoir's state matrix.

 \textbf{Noise} $\xi_t$: Noise introduces stochastic perturbations to the reservoir state. Noise insertion has been shown to improve attractor stability in echo state networks by preventing trajectories from collapsing into undesired fixed points or oscillatory modes, though this comes at the cost of reduced prediction precision \parencite{jaeger_echo_state_2001}. Because the present experiments focus on task-agnostic structural invariance rather than attractor training, noise is not included as a feature of our model.

\chapter{Background}

\section{The C. elegans Connectome}
\subsection{Historical Justification}
In 1966, the mathematician and psychologist Seymour Papert initiated the ``Summer Vision Project,'' an early attempt to develop programs capable of performing image-classification tasks. The project revealed that visual perception was far more difficult to formalize computationally than early researchers had anticipated, and that heuristic approaches were insufficient for the problem. \parencite{nilsson_2010}

Earlier, in 1962, Hubel and Wiesel studied the visual cortex of cats and found neurons that responded selectively to oriented bars of light at specific locations, as well as more complex cells that responded to such features across a range of positions. These results suggested that biological vision achieves robust computation through layered feature extraction.\parencite{lindsay_2020}

Fukushima drew directly on these biological principles in 1980 when he developed the Neocognitron, a multilayer neural architecture capable of image classification that was robust to positional shifts. The Neocognitron is widely regarded as a precursor to modern convolutional neural networks. This historical arc is instructive: when computational principles proved inadequate, progress came from studying biological systems and abstracting structural principles from them. \parencite{fukushima_1980} 

The same logic motivates the present work. Biological neural networks must function reliably across noisy, variable, and changing conditions, making them natural models of robust computation. The gap between artificial and biological robustness is still visible in many domains: engineered systems often remain brittle outside their training regime\parencite{recht2019,geirhos_jacobsen_michaelis_zemel_brendel_bethge_wichmann_2020}, whereas animals routinely generalize behavior across unfamiliar environments \parencite{burden_libby_jayaram_sponberg_donelan_2024,pearson_2000}.

For that reason, this thesis turns to a biological network not to explain the organism itself, but to study what its architecture may reveal about robust computation. We therefore use the \textit{C.\ elegans} connectome as a network architecture prior and investigate which of its structural features contribute to hyperparameter-invariant computation.
\subsection{Connectome}
A connectome is a comprehensive structural map of the neural wiring of an organism. It encodes which neurons are connected, the direction of said connection, and often additional biological metadata such as the number of synaptic terminals and the neurotransmitter associated with each connection.

In this work, we treat the connectome as the foundation for a graph: each neuron is represented as a node, and each synapse as a directed, weighted edge. The  connectome used in our study is sourced from the c302 project\parencite{c302}, which is an initiative to model the neural network of the nematode \textit{C. elegans}.
The network is composed of several key components:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Neurons}: The database includes all 302 identified neurons in the \textit{C. elegans} nervous system.
    Our model includes 299 neurons; CANL, CANR, VC6 are omitted (see Sec.~\ref{sec:omission}). 
    \item \textbf{Synaptic connections}: The c302 connectome model contains 3672 synaptic connections but 564 them are neuromuscular junctions; sending neurotransmitters to muscle cells and are not included in our graph. This leaves us with 3108 synaptic connections, categorized into various types based on the nature of the synapse, including chemical synapses and gap junctions. Specifically, we assign inhibitory synapses (GABAergic) negative weights (190 synapses) and excitatory synapses--acetylcholine (394 synapses) and glutamate(962)--positive weights. All remaining transmitter (1562 synapses) are assigned positive sign. This sign assignment is a \emph{modeling prior} used to define an excitatory/inhibitory partition for the invariance experiments; it does not attempt to capture receptor-specific or context-dependent effects (e.g., cases where acetylcholine or glutamate can be inhibitory). Because our study analyzes how coarse sign structure and weight statistics affect task-agnostic reservoir metrics under hyperparameter sweeps, incorporating detailed synapse-type dynamics is outside scope. We retain the non-GABA synapses (rather than dropping them) to preserve the observed network density and recurrence, which strongly influence reservoir dynamics and the metrics studied here.

    
    The connections are represented as directed edges in the network, indicating the direction of signal transmission.
    Gap junctions are considered to be two distinct edges with independent weights for computational simplicity.
\item \textbf{Connection count (proxy weights)}: The dataset reports only the number of terminal contacts that neuron $i$ makes onto neuron $j$, not graded synaptic strengths. We therefore use that contact count $c_{ij}$ as an initial weight, $W_{ij}=c_{ij}$. Treating contact number as a proxy weight is standard in \emph{C.\ elegans} modeling \parencite{c302,Casal2020} and provides the biologically informed prior for our training.

\end{itemize}
\subsection{Omission of CANL/CANR}
\textbf{\emph{Omission of CANL/CANR.}}
\label{sec:omission}
In the c302 wiring used here, CANL and CANR form gap-junction contacts only with excretory tissues and have no neuronal synapses. Because our node set includes neurons and muscles but not excretory cells, including CANL/CANR would produce two isolated vertices (no neuronal in- or out-edges and no neuromuscular outputs). Additionally, VC6 is a forms an outgoing connection to a muscle, but has no incoming or outgoing synapses to or from other neurons. Under the dynamics in Sec.~\ref{sec:isolated}, isolated nodes are decoupled from the controller and cannot influence motor commands or reward; their inclusion/exclusion is therefore behaviorally invariant in this model.

\subsection{Isolated nodes}
\label{sec:isolated}
Nodes with no incoming or outgoing synaptic connections are dynamically inert and therefore do not influence dynamics of the network. Formally, if neuron $i$ satisfies $W_{ij}=0$ and $W_{ji}=0$ for all $j$, then its state does not contribute to the state update of any other neuron. Consequently, adding or removing such isolated nodes does not alter the dynamics of the remaining network.

This condition applies to CANL, CANR, and VC6 in the c302-derived graph, as their known synaptic contacts are exclusively with excretory (CANL, CANR) and vulval muscles (VC6), which are omitted from the modeled neuron set. Consequently, these neurons are isolated within the weight matrix and do not influence the reservoir dynamics.

\section{Task Agnostic Network Statistics}
\subsection{Motivation}
What makes a given neural circuit computationally powerful? More precisely, how can two network architectures be compared across a suite of tasks without explicitly training them on those tasks? In reservoir computing, this question is addressed through task-agnostic statistics that quantify intrinsic dynamical properties of the network. Measures such as information processing capacity (IPC), kernel rank (KR), generalization rank (GR), and memory capacity (MC) characterize complementary functional dimensions, including nonlinearity, separability, generalization, and fading memory. Additionally, these statistics are widely used as benchmarking tools for reservoir networks \parencite{pilati_ceni_michieletti_gallicchio_ricciardi_milano_2026}.

\subsection{Kernel Rank}
Returning to the pool described earlier, we can use this picture to build intuition for the task-agnostic network statistics considered in this work. One could also drop different sized stones sequentially at different places in the pool. They could do this experiment multiple times; observing how distinct the resulting wave states are. Kernel rank is a measure of how distinctly the pool responds to different sets of inputs; if many input sequences lead to the same wave patterns, then the kernel rank will be low. If those input sequences instead lead(\textit{map}) to distinct wave patterns then the kernel rank will be high.

Kernel rank measures how effectively inputs are projected into a high-dimensional state space so they become linearly separable by a readout $W^{out}$, serving as a measurable proxy for kernel quality and the diversity of nonlinear transformations created by the reservoir\parencite{Stepney2024PRCtutorial}. Higher is better for this metric. Specifically it is a measure of the rank of the $n \times m$  matrix whose columns are the internal state ($x_{u_i}(t_0)$) at some fixed time $t_0$ for the preceding input stream $u_i$. Where $n$ is the number of units in the circuit, $m$ is the number of input streams, ($x_{u_i}(t_0)$) is the internal state at time $t_0$, we have $m$ different input streams $u_i$. This is the original definition\parencite{maass_comp_power_2004}, we will use a modified version of this also found in literature \parencite{Stepney2024PRCtutorial}. In our implementation, we use the state-matrix variant common in benchmarking: we drive the reservoir with a single i.i.d. input sequence $u_t$---rather than m input streams---and collect the post-washout state matrix $X \in \mathbb{R}^{T\times N}$ where each row is the reservoir state at each time step. Kernel Rank is computed using effective rank \parencite{roy_vetterli_2007}. Because the readout is linear, neurons with very similar activity patterns provide redundant information. We want a kernel whose neurons' activity patterns vary greatly, such that this can be utilized by our readout. Thus we need a different measure of rank, as matrix rank only detects exact linear dependence; neurons with highly similar but not identical activity patterns will still increase the rank. We want a measure of rank that measures variance across orthogonal modes. This leads us to effective rank. To compute effective rank, we perform the following steps:\\
First we center our values with respect to time\\
\[ X_c = X-\bar{X}\] \\
where \[\bar{X}_{tj} = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t'=1}^{T}X_{t'j}\] This defines an  $\mathbb{R}^{T\times N}$ matrix where every row is the mean neural activity of a neuron over time.\\
Get the singular values from singular value decomposition
\[
S = [\sigma_1,\sigma_2,...\sigma_n] = \text{SVDVALS}(X_c)
\]
Traditionally, the values of a singular value decomposition represent the scaling in each orthogonal dimension. When we are getting the $\sigma$s, we do so by obtaining the square root of the eigen values of $X_c^TX_c$. $X_c^TX_c$ is the unnormalized covariance matrix of $X_c$. A large magnitude at $X_c^TX_c$ at index $i,j$ means that neuron $i$'s activity is closely aligned with neuron $j$'s. A low value indicates a weak linear correlation between their signals.\\
The eigenvalues describe how much variance lies along each orthogonal activity pattern. If many neurons behave similarly, most variance lies in a small number of directions and only a few eigenvalues are large. If neurons exhibit diverse activity patterns, variance spreads across more directions and more eigenvalues become significant.\\

Next, ensure that there are no $\sigma$ with value 0 by setting all that are equal to 0 to $\epsilon$, we use $\epsilon = 1e-12$.
\[
S'_{i} = \max(\epsilon,S_{i}) 
\]
Now we get the "probabilities" of each of the singular values. We divide by the sum, so that $\sum p_i = 1$ and so that we can use Shannon entropy.
\[
p_i = \frac{S'_{i}}{\sum^N_{k=1}{S'_{k}}}
\]
We are invoking Shannon entropy to tell us how much information on average we need to encode our singular values. If there is one large singular values with the rest being close to 0 then we need substantially less information to encode our distribution than if there is multiple singular values with similar sizes. 
\[
H= -\sum_{i=1}^{N} (p_i\ln{p_i})
\]
then we do $e^H$ so that we can get the effective rank of our matrix. Take $p_i =\frac{1}{k}$, then H = $\ln{{k}}$ thus $e^H = k$ which is the effective rank of our matrix.
\[
\text{erank} = e^{H}
\]

\subsection{Generalization Rank}
Returning to the pool, suppose we wanted to observe how much the waves patterns change if we used slightly larger or smaller rocks. We could alter the size of our rocks slightly and drop them at slightly different places. By comparing the resulting wave pattern to the wave pattern of the unaltered rocks, we can measure how many independent wave patterns the pool produces from these nearly identical inputs. If small changes in the stones lead to many distinct wave patterns, the pool is highly sensitive and maps to a high-dimensional set of states; if small changes lead to similar wave patterns, the pool generalizes and collapses to a lower-dimensional set of states. This is measured by the Generalization Rank.

Generalization Rank (GR) quantifies the sensitivity of reservoir states to small perturbations in the input. It is computed as the effective rank of the difference between state trajectories driven by clean and noise-perturbed inputs. Lower GR indicates that input perturbations induce lower-dimensional deviations in state space, reflecting greater robustness and reduced sensitivity to noise.
\[
u_t \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} \mathcal{U}(-1,1)
\]
\[
u'_t = u_t + \kappa \,\varepsilon_t, \qquad \varepsilon_t \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0,1).
\]
with $\kappa$ in our version being set by 0.01.
\\
then we run the network with the perurbed $u'$, this network will have states $X'$ and with the unperturbed $u$, with states $X$. 

\[
\text{Generalization Rank} = \text{erank}(X'-X)
\]

if our network generalizes well, small amounts of noise on the input will not change the internal state after a set washout period.


\subsection{Memory Capacity}
Returning to the pool analogy, suppose we wish to infer the size of a stone after it has been dropped into the water. The stone itself is no longer visible, but its presence is encoded in the resulting wave pattern. By observing the waves, one could estimate the size of the stone. If multiple stones are dropped sequentially, their wave patterns overlap and interfere. The older a stone, the more its influence dissipates and mixes with subsequent disturbances.

In reservoir computing, the input signal plays the role of the stones, and the reservoir state plays the role of the wave pattern. Memory capacity measures how well the current reservoir state can \textit{reconstruct} past inputs at varying delays.

Memory capacity, introduced by Jaeger \parencite{Jaeger2002STMESN}, is a measure of the amount of linear memory a reservoir contains. Given an input $u(t)\in U[-1,1]$, the reservoir's memory capacity is determined by its ability to reproduce this input at at  varying delays ($k$). Memory capacity is originally defined by Jaeger as   
\[\text{MC}_k=\dfrac{\big(\text{cov}\!\left(u(t-k),\hat u_k(t)\right)\big)^2}{\text{var}(u)\,\text{var}(\hat u_k)}\] \[\text{MC}=\sum_{k=1}^\infty\text{MC}_k.\]\

$MC_k$ measures the correlation between the predicted signal and the original signal or how well the network "remembers" the original signal at time $k$. Summing over $k$ gives us how well the network can remember a signal over all delays $k$. As $k$ increases the $MC_k$ typically decreases, this is intuitive, as we are in a network that has the echo state property (the state of the network is determined by its input history and not by its initial state). Hence we try to recreate the input $u_{t-k}$ from the state $x_t$, but because our network has the echo state property, the state, $x_{t-k}$ resulting from the input $u_{t-k}$ loses influence over $x_t$ as $k$ grows. Jaeger also shows that for i.i.d. (independent and
identically distributed) random input, $MC \leq N$ \parencite{Jaeger2002STMESN,Stepney2024PRCtutorial}.\\

Let $X' = \frac{X-\bar{X}}{\text{std}(X)+\epsilon}$,
$\xi$ is a user specified parameter denoting the strength of L2 regression,
and
$I \in R^{N \times N}$. $Y' = Y_{\text{train}}$\footnote{We use $Y'=Y$ the typical equation has Y = $\frac{Y-\bar{Y}}{\text{std}(Y)+\epsilon}$ we can skip this for a computational speedup by knowing that $\bar{Y}\approx0$ hence $Y' = \frac{Y}{\text{std}(Y)+\epsilon}$, however this can be written as $Y'=aY$ with $a=\frac{1}{\text{std}(Y)+\epsilon}$ we can just multiply the weight matrix found from ridge regularization by a, this is automatically done by ridge regression.}

We define 
\[
\text{Ridge\_Regression}(X_{\text{train}},Y_{\text{train}},\xi)= ((X')^TX'+\xi I)^{-1}(X')^TY'
\]




Jaeger's definition of $MC$ is the Pearson correlation coefficient squared, which is equal to R-squared. R-squared or the coefficient of determination is a measure of how much of the variation in the data is explained by our predictions. The numerator is the residual sum of squares. The denominator is the total sum of squares. Here, $Y$ is the true input signal, $\hat{Y}$ is the predicted input signal, $\bar{Y}$ is the average of $Y$, and $\epsilon$ is a tiny value to ensure that we do not divide by 0.
\[
R^2_{\text{score}}(Y,\hat{Y}) =1- \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{T} (Y_i-\hat{Y}_i)^2}{\sum^T_{i=1}(Y_i-\bar{Y})^2+\epsilon}
\]




We define memory capacity as
\[
\mathrm{MC}
=
\sum_{d=1}^{D_{\max}}
\max\!\bigl(0.0,\;R^2_{\mathrm{score}}(Y_d,\hat{Y_d})\bigr),
\]
where
\[
Y_d = u^{\mathrm{test}}_{0:T_{te}-d},
\qquad
\hat{Y_d}
=
X^{\mathrm{test}}_{d:T_{te}}
\,\mathrm{Ridge\_Regression}\!\left(
X^{\mathrm{train}}_{d:T_{tr}},
\;
u^{\mathrm{train}}_{0:T_{tr}-d}, \xi
\right).
\]

where $D_{\max}$ is the maximum delay included in the sum; in our experiments, we use $D_{\max}=30$. This departs from the formal definition of memory capacity, which sums over all delays, but an infinite sum is not computationally feasible in practice. Furthermore, $MC_k$ decays as $k \to \infty$, so terms at sufficiently large delays contribute negligibly.

To validate this truncation, we swept $D_{\max} \in \{30,50,80\}$ across all 14 models, using 20 random seeds per model (280 evaluations in total), and treated $D_{\max}=80$ as the reference condition. Across these evaluations, $D_{\max}=30$ captured an average of $100.00\%$ of the memory capacity measured at $D_{\max}=80$, with a standard deviation of $0.02\%$, a minimum of $99.71\%$, and a 5th percentile of $100.00\%$. Model-wise agreement with the $D_{\max}=80$ reference was also perfect: for both $D_{\max}=30$ and $D_{\max}=50$, the across-model mean capture was $100.00\%$, and the corresponding model rankings were also unchanged, with Pearson $r=1.0000$ across all 14 models.

We therefore use $D_{\max}=30$ throughout, since it preserves essentially the full observed memory capacity while being more computationally efficient than larger cutoffs.

\section{Invariance across Hyper-parameters}
Variance measures the degree of spread in a data set from its mean value. In this study we are measuring variance of key network statistics (GR,KR,IPC,MC) under hyperparameter change. It is important to note that while the values of these network statistics are important, i.e. a high IPC being an indicator of a strong network architecture, we are investigating how these values change under hyperparameter change compared between different model architecture, rather than the values themselves. Hence we will not measure variance but rather we will measure coefficient of variation, as this provides a measure of variance across different scales. \[CV = \frac{\text{std}(a)}{\bar{a}}\]

Where a is the set of values of the network statistic we are measuring, i.e. IPC, MC, GR, or KR, under a set of differing hyperparameter values. It is desirable to have a low CV, as this indicates that the network statistic is invariant to hyperparameter change, meaning the network can be trained with a wide range of hyper-parameters without changing the network's ability to perform a given task.

\section{Network topology priors}
\subsection{Small-world networks}

The small-world phenomenon refers to the observation that the distance between two randomly chosen nodes in a network is surprisingly short, even when the network contains many nodes \parencite{network_science}. Small-world networks are characterized by the coexistence of two structural properties: a high clustering coefficient and a short characteristic path length.

Formally, let $\overline{L}$ denote the average path length, the mean shortest paths distance between all pairs of nodes. Let $\overline{C}$ denote the average clustering coefficient of the network, defined as the mean degree to which the neighbors of a given node connect to each other. To quantify small-world structure, these quantities are typically normalized relative to an equivalent random network with the same number of nodes and edges, denoted $\overline{L}_{\mathrm{rand}}$ and $\overline{C}_{\mathrm{rand}}$, respectively. The small-worldness metric is then defined as
\[
S
=
\frac{\overline{C}/\overline{C}_{\mathrm{rand}}}
{\overline{L}/\overline{L}_{\mathrm{rand}}}.
\]
A network is considered small-world when $S > 1$, indicating that it maintains higher clustering than a random network while preserving similarly short path lengths.\parencite{humphries_gurney_2008}

\subsection{Small-World Topology Enhances the Echo State Property and Signal Propagation}

Kawai et al. \parencite{KAWAI201915} demonstrated that networks with small-world structure~\parencite{Wssmallworld} (\(p = 0.1\)) exhibit enhanced memory capacity in reservoir computing when input and output nodes were spatially segregated. They localized input nodes in one region of the network and output nodes in a distant region. Small-world networks achieved a larger total memory capacity, quantified as
\[
\sum_{\rho(W_{\mathrm{res}})} \mathrm{MC},
\] compared to networks not exhibiting the small-world property.

Although fully random networks (\(p = 1\)) achieved the single highest peak memory capacity—at a spectral radius slightly below one—small-world networks maintained strong performance across a broader range of spectral radii. In contrast, the performance of fully random reservoirs fell sharply outside of a narrow band of spectral radii.

This result provides conceptual motivation for the present work: a biologically inspired architecture with small-world structure can exhibit comparatively stable computational performance across spectral radii, rather than relying on precise tuning near the edge of stability, as is often assumed in traditional reservoir computing theory.\footnote{Here, "traditional" refers to the common assumption in reservoir computing that fully random connectivity maximizes dynamical diversity and therefore computational ability.}

\subsubsection{C.~elegans--Matched Watts--Strogatz Model with Gaussian Weights} %has fig

To see if small-worldness has an effect on task-agnostic invariance we employed the model introduced by \parencite{Wssmallworld}. The procedure begins with a a lattice-like regular network of \(N\) nodes, where each node is connected to its \(k\) nearest neighbors. Each edge is then independently rewired with probability \(p\): for an edge \((u,v)\), the endpoint \(v\) is reassigned to a randomly selected vertex \(v'\), avoiding self-loops and duplicate edges.

When \(p = 0\), the network remains a regular lattice, characterized by high clustering and long characteristic path length. When \(p = 1\), the network approaches a random graph, exhibiting low clustering and short path length. For intermediate values of \(p\), typically near \(p \approx 0.1\), the network displays the defining features of small-world structure: high clustering combined with short characteristic path length.

To match the scale of the \emph{C.~elegans} connectome, we generated Watts--Strogatz graphs with \(N = 299\) nodes. We set the rewiring probability to \(p = 0.1\) to induce small-world structure and chose an initial neighborhood size of \(k = 10\). In the Watts--Strogatz construction the total number of edges is determined by \(N \times k\). Because \(k\) must be an integer and \(N\) is fixed at the empirical neuron count of \(299\), the Watts--Strogatz model cannot exactly reproduce the empirical connectome's edge count of \(3108\). We therefore chose \(k\) to minimize the difference in total edge count between the model and the biological network. For \(N = 299\), this gives \(k = 10\), yielding a graph with \(2990\) edges.

After fixing the topology, synaptic weights were independently assigned to the remaining edges by sampling from a Gaussian distribution,

\section{Trial definition}
A single trial of a model consists of:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item a reservoir instantiation for each hyperparameter combination
    \item a performance evaluation for each hyperparameter combination. Using the following parameters \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Sequence Length} = 2000
        \item \textbf{Washout time steps} = 500
        \item \textbf{Perturb STD}  = 0.01 (for calculating GR)
        \item \textbf{Train time steps} = 1500
        \item \textbf{Test time steps} = 500
        \item \textbf{Memory capacity Max delay}
        \item \textbf{Information processing capacity max delay}
        \item \textbf{Information processing capacity max polynomial order}
        \item \textbf{Ridge Alpha} = 0.0001 (controls the strength of L2 regularization for ridge regression)
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}
The coefficient of variation (CV) is computed across hyperparameter combinations within a trial. Each trial uses a single fixed input sequence shared across all hyperparameter combinations to ensure comparability. Reservoir instantiations are independent across hyperparameter combinations: for each combination, the reservoir is reinitialized with a unique seed. Each reservoir instantiation in a trial uses the same input sequence, different trials use different input sequences.

Number of trials for histogram experiment ~\ref{fig:arch}: 500 per model. Total metric evaluations per model: $\text{Trials} \times \text {hyperparameter grid} = 500 \times 4\times3\times4 = 24,000$. Number of trials for ~\ref{fig:er_frac_curves} and Figures~\ref{fig:bin_cel_frac_curves},~\ref{fig:er_frac_curves},~\ref{fig:og_cel_frac_curves}: 250 per point. Total metric evaluations per point: $250 \times 4\times3\times4 = 12,000$. Number of trials for ~\ref{fig:weights} and ~\ref{fig:weights}: 50 per point. Total metric evaluations per point: $50 \times 4\times3\times4 = 2,400$.

\section{Equivalence testing}
We use two one-sided tests (TOST) to test whether the difference in mean CV between architectures lies within equivalence bounds. Equivalence bounds are defined as:

\[
\Delta = \eta \cdot \mathrm{median}(CV_{\mathrm{all}})
\]

We test:

\[
H_0: |\mu_1 - \mu_2| \ge \Delta
\]

\[
H_1: |\mu_1 - \mu_2| < \Delta
\]

Equivalence is concluded if both one-sided tests reject $H_0$ at $\eta$ = 0.05.

\section{Invariance to degree-preserving topology changes}

To test whether hyperparameter invariance depends on connectivity itself or on the distribution of weights carried by that connectivity, we consider three shuffling experiments. All three preserve single-node degree, but they differ in underlying weight distributions. Taken together, these experiments separate the effects of topological shuffles from the effects of weight shuffles.

The first experiment, shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:realW}, Table~\ref{tab:tost_grkr_shuffles_1}, and Table~\ref{tab:tost_mcipc_shuffles_1}, uses the original \textit{C.\ elegans} weight matrix. Here, both weight shuffling and connection shuffling increase the coefficient of variation (CV), indicating reduced invariance under hyperparameter sweeps. Crucially, however, these two manipulations increase CV by nearly the same amount. The combined weight+connection shuffle serves as a control for whether these effects are additive. If weight shuffling and connection shuffling disrupted invariance through distinct mechanisms, then applying both transformations together should have produced a larger increase in CV than either transformation alone. Instead, the combined shuffle matches the weight-shuffle and connection-shuffle conditions across all four task-agnostic metrics. Consistent with this visual overlap, the pairwise comparisons among weight shuffle, connection shuffle, and weight+connection shuffle were equivalent across metrics. This suggests that the increase in CV produced by connection shuffling is not primarily a topological effect. Rather, in the real-weight setting, connection shuffling matters because it disrupts the original assignment of heterogeneous biological weights to edges. The lower CV of the unshuffled \textit{C.\ elegans} network therefore indicates that this biological weight arrangement is invariance-promoting relative to the shuffle controls tested here, though these experiments do not establish that it is globally CV-minimizing. %%though this would be an interesting question for future work.

The second experiment, shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:pm1W}, Table~\ref{tab:tost_grkr_shuffles_2}, and Table~\ref{tab:tost_mcipc_shuffles_2}, repeats the same analysis after collapsing all nonzero weights to $\pm 1$. This removes magnitude heterogeneity while preserving sign structure. Under this constraint, weight shuffle, connection shuffle, and weight+connection shuffle have little effect on GR and KR, and pairwise comparisons for these metrics are equivalent. IPC is also largely stable, with 5 out of 6 pairewise comparisons being equivalent. MC behaves differently: weight shuffling increases CV, whereas connection shuffle and connection+weight shuffle reduce it relative to the baseline. Correspondingly, equivalence across the four models largely breaks down for MC. The main result of this experiment is therefore metric-specific. Once magnitude heterogeneity is removed, GR and KR remain largely invariant to both rewiring and sign redistribution, whereas MC remains sensitive both to which connections are present in the graph and to how $\pm 1$ weights are distributed across those connections. IPC lies between these cases: more stable than MC, but not as fully preserved as GR and KR.

This second experiment also tests whether invariance depends on the topological placement of excitatory and inhibitory synapses, or instead on the global balance of neurotransmitter signs. To isolate this factor, we compare the $\pm 1$ model that preserves edge sign to the $\pm 1$ model that preserves the global sign distribution. In this model, the overall proportion of excitatory and inhibitory weights is preserved, but their locations are randomized. The equivalence of these two models across 3 out of 4 metrics (see Table~\ref{tab:tost_grkr_shuffles_2} and Table~\ref{tab:tost_mcipc_shuffles_2}) suggests that the specific topological arrangement of excitatory and inhibitory synapses is not a key determinant of invariance. Instead, invariance appears to depend more on global dynamical constraints imposed by sign balance, rather than on the micro-scale structure of individual synapse polarities. 

The third experiment, shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:bW} and Table~\ref{tab:tost_grkr_shuffles_3}, isolates topology more directly by using binary weights. In this setting, all nonzero edges have identical magnitude and sign, so shuffling weights is trivial and has no effect. The only meaningful perturbation is therefore a degree-preserving connection shuffle. Under this manipulation, the shuffled-topology model and the binary base are equivalent for GR and KR, but not for IPC and MC, where CV decreases significantly after rewiring. This finding is important because it rules out the idea that the earlier effects of connection shuffling arose solely th the accompanying shuffle of heterogeneous weights. Even when that possibility is removed, IPC and MC still respond to topology, whereas GR and KR remain comparatively insensitive.

\section{Equivalence testing}
Equivalence testing using TOST (Table~\ref{tab:tost_mcipc} and ~\ref{tab:tost_grkr} ) further clarifies these relationships. We used TOST equivalence testing on the mean dispersion (CV) between each architecture and the biological connectome. The equivalence margin was set to $\Delta = |0.05(\text{median}(\text{dispersion}))|$ computed per metric accross all runs. We concluded equivalence when both one-sided tests rejected the null that the mean difference lay outside $[-\Delta,+\Delta]$ Thus, small p-values indicate that the dispersion difference is statistically confined within the predefined practical tolerance. Under this criterion, only specific sign-preserving perturbations satisfied equivalence for selected metrics, while most architectures—including connection shuffles, weight shuffles, and random graph controls—did not satisfy equivalence across all metrics simultaneously. Failure to conclude equivalence (large $p$) does not imply non-equivalence; it indicates insufficient evidence that the mean difference is within $\pm\Delta$ under the chosen margin and sample size. 1000 obersvations for each comparison.
